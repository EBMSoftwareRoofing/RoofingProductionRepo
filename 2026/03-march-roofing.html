The Monthly High-Five

The moves that matter from March 2026.

M&A
QXO Closes $2.25B Kodiak Deal — Eyes $50B Empire by 2031
QXO closed its acquisition of Kodiak Building Partners in late March, combining Beacon's roofing/exterior platform with Kodiak's $2.4B lumber, trusses, gypsum and structural products base. Valued at ~10.7x projected 2025 EBITDA (7.3x with synergies). QXO now eyes Boise Cascade, BlueLinx and US LBM as next targets with ~$10B in remaining war chest.
⚡ QXO's addressable market just expanded to $200B+. The consolidation playbook is no longer just about roofing.

Materials
Major Manufacturers Push Coordinated Price Increases — Effective Late March/April
Atlas Roofing (5–8%), CertainTeed (up to 8%), and TAMKO announced price increases on shingles, underlayment and ventilation — effective March 23–April 15, 2026, regardless of prior order entry date. All cited rising production input costs. Aluminum mill shapes are up 33% YoY per AGC; steel mill products rose 20.7% YoY as of January 2026.
⚡ Contractors with open bids from pre-March proposals are absorbing margin gaps in real time. Lock in material pricing before Q2 deliveries.

Regulatory
DOL Moves to Rescind 2024 Independent Contractor Rule
In late February — confirmed via March legal roundup — the DOL's Wage and Hour Division proposed rescinding the 2024 independent contractor rule, returning toward the narrower 2021 standard. This shift favors a broader definition of independent contractor status, potentially reducing W-2 reclassification risk for subcontractor-heavy platforms.
⚡ Welcome relief for PE platforms with 1099-heavy labor models — but "pending" means nothing is locked. Continue modeling both scenarios.
4
PE Activity
Midwest Roll-Up Activity Accelerates: Royalty Roofing, Ridgeline & More
March saw continued bolt-on activity in the Midwest: Royalty Roofing USA expanded its commercial footprint by acquiring Division 7 across Michigan; Ridgeline Roofing & Restoration acquired Bold North Roofing in the Twin Cities/western Wisconsin market. Both moves reflect geographic density strategies prioritized by PE buyers — particularly in high-storm-frequency corridors.
⚡ Midwest density plays are accelerating ahead of spring storm season. Target-rich geography meets non-discretionary demand cycle.
5
Supply Chain
Construction Input Prices Up 2.9% YoY — Spring Season Pricing Pressure Builds
ABC/BLS PPI data through January 2026 showed nonresidential construction input prices up 2.9% YoY — with an annualized January rate ABC describes as a "blistering" 7.1%. Freight costs forecast to rise 4–6% in 2026. Spring (March–May) is peak pricing season, with contractors entering bid season into a rising-cost environment with no material relief in sight.
⚡ Operators without fixed-price supplier contracts are entering spring with exposed margins. Procurement strategy is now a competitive advantage.
Retrospective

Lessons from the Ladder

History this industry learned from — or repeats.

The First Roll-Up Wave: What They Got Wrong (Late 1990s–Early 2000s)

The first institutional wave imposed uniform labor models on hyper-local markets and centralized procurement before systems existed to manage it. They paid premium multiples for revenue they couldn't retain post-transition. Result: value destruction, operator exodus after earnout, and a generation of PE skeptics in the trades.

Platforms that survived share one trait: they bought the operator as much as the business — retaining founders with real equity and building tech infrastructure before they needed it.

🎓 Earnout structures that ignore workforce continuity are the sector's #1 value destroyer.

Katrina (2005) & the 2011 Super Outbreak

Katrina: Gulf Coast labor spiked 40–60% in 90 days. Lead times stretched 6+ months. Undercapitalized shops couldn't manage materials, crews, and insurance timelines simultaneously — a demand surge that ruined the unprepared.

2011: 358 tornadoes, $11B insured losses. PE investments in AL/TN/MS in 2012–13 were acquired at peak storm-cycle multiples. When demand normalized, the underlying business was smaller than the thesis assumed.

🎓 Storm EBITDA ≠ recurring EBITDA. Always normalize the trailing twelve.

March Tech Spotlight

Things To Watch: EagleView One

Each month we spotlight one tool, platform, or technology worth understanding — whether you're an operator, a PE investor evaluating platforms, or both.

If you've been in roofing for more than a few years, you know EagleView as the company that produces the aerial measurement reports your estimators order before every job. That description needs updating. In March 2026, EagleView formally launched EagleView One — a unified, subscription-based platform that combines high-precision aerial imagery, AI-powered analytics, and full 3D property modeling into a single interface accessible from any device. The company is positioning it not as a reporting tool but as a property intelligence platform — and the distinction matters.

The headline capability is 3D exterior measurement at 98.77% claimed accuracy, now covering not just roofs but walls, windows, and doors for both residential and commercial properties. For contractors, that means a single platform generating complete exterior scope — not just the roof report. For PE platforms managing multi-crew operations across geographies, it means standardized measurement inputs that survive crew and estimator turnover. EagleView's CEO Piers Dormeyer put it plainly: the goal is to be the single source of truth for every property-related workflow, from estimate to insurance claim to predictive maintenance scheduling.

The March 12th live showcase event introduced EagleView One's virtual collaboration features — multiple stakeholders accessing and manipulating the same 3D property model simultaneously. Think adjuster, contractor, and property owner reviewing the same damage documentation in real time, without anyone climbing a ladder. EagleView also announced the launch of EagleView Labs, a dedicated AI innovation hub, and appointed Dr. Dylan Kesler to lead AI research — signaling that the product roadmap is investment-backed, not marketing theater.

What it measures: TRIR (Total Recordable Incident Rate) counts OSHA-recordable injuries and illnesses per 100 full-time equivalent employees over a 12-month period. The formula: (number of recordable incidents × 200,000) ÷ total hours worked. The 2023 construction industry average sits at 2.3 — a useful baseline, but roofing contractors typically run higher given fall exposure and seasonal labor churn. Sub-1.0 is achievable and recognized with safety awards; above 3.0 starts triggering bid prequalification scrutiny.

Why PE buyers care: TRIR feeds directly into workers' comp premiums, general liability rates, and bonding capacity — three cost centers that can swing platform EBITDA by 200–400 basis points. A target running above 3.0 is paying a safety tax on every job and may already be flagged in contractor prequalification databases. Below 2.3 — at or better than the construction average — signals a management team that enforces process. Below 1.0 signals a top-tier safety culture that survives ownership transition. Diligence teams now pull OSHA 300 logs as standard Q&A, not just to assess liability exposure, but to read the operational culture.

March 2026 relevance: Spring ramp-up is the highest-risk period in roofing. Crews scaling from winter skeleton schedules, new seasonal hires without full training cycles, and weather transitions create the conditions for incident spikes. A platform sitting near 2.3 entering Q2 has little margin for error — any spring spike pushes them toward the 3.0 bid-scrutiny threshold. Rising incident rates in Q2 feed directly into insurance renewal conversations in Q3 and Q4, exactly when PE platforms are often in mid-diligence or preparing for exit.

Watch-out: TRIR can be gamed by under-reporting — a crew lead who discourages incident logging to protect a bonus is destroying your safety record and your legal standing simultaneously. Pair TRIR with near-miss reporting rates: a healthy safety culture generates near-miss reports at roughly 10× the TRIR. If your near-miss log is empty and your TRIR looks clean at 2.3 or below, your data is almost certainly wrong.

The Square Foot Bottom Line

March delivered a clear message: the consolidation window is compressing from both ends simultaneously. Above the market, QXO closed Kodiak and now controls a $200B+ addressable market with ~$10B still to deploy — and it has named its next targets. Inside the market, Atlas, CertainTeed, and TAMKO executed coordinated 5–8% price increases effective the final week of March, hitting every contractor with open bids and no locked-in material pricing. The operators squeezed hardest are the ones with neither a procurement strategy nor the tech stack to reprice estimates in real time.

Meanwhile, the DOL's move to rescind the 2024 independent contractor rule — confirmed in March's legal roundup — offers PE platforms a potential reprieve on W-2 reclassification exposure. That is not permission to ignore the issue. It is permission to get your documentation in order while the regulatory window is favorable. The rule is pending, not resolved.

The operators entering Q2 in the strongest position are those who spent March doing three things: locking material pricing before March effective dates, pulling OSHA 300 logs to get ahead of any TRIR exposure before spring incident season peaks, and closing the gap between their estimating cycle and the market's repricing cycle. Everyone else is reacting. Know your TRIR before a buyer's diligence team requests your OSHA 300 logs.

Riddle Answer

Answer: Pitch. In roofing, pitch (slope) determines drainage and structural integrity — too flat and you get ponding water; steep is better. In private equity, a "pitch" is the investor presentation, and a steeper growth story signals a more compelling multiple. Same word. Entirely different anxieties — and somehow, the same consequences when you get it wrong.
