April 2026 Market Snapshot

The Monthly High-Five

The moves that matter from April 2026.

M&A
QXO Announces $17B TopBuild Deal — Days After Closing $2.25B Kodiak
QXO closed its $2.25B acquisition of Kodiak Building Partners on April 1, then announced a definitive agreement to acquire TopBuild Corp. (NYSE: BLD) for ~$17B on April 19 at $505/share — a 23.1% premium to TopBuild's April 17 close, valued at ~14.9x 2025 EBITDA pre-synergies (11.8x post-synergies). Combined company: ~1,150 locations, ~28,000 employees, >$18B revenue, >$2B adjusted EBITDA. QXO becomes #1 in insulation and waterproofing and #2 in roofing in North America.
⚡ In 30 days QXO put $19B+ to work. Roofing distribution is now a duopoly conversation between QXO and Home Depot/SRS. With ~$6B remaining per BNP Paribas, the consolidation runway isn't done.
2
Materials
March Price Increases Hit Q2 Deliveries — Margins Compressed Across Open Bids
Atlas Roofing (5–8%), CertainTeed (up to 8%), and TAMKO price increases that took effect March 23–April 15 are now fully reflected in April material orders, regardless of original bid date. Combined with construction input prices up 2.9% YoY (ABC/BLS PPI through January, annualized rate of 7.1%) and freight forecast 4–6% higher in 2026, contractors who didn't lock procurement before March are absorbing the gap on every job booked in April.
⚡ Spring's margin damage is now showing up in real production data. February bids being installed with April materials — that's the gap eating Q2 EBITDA.
3
Regulatory
DOL Independent Contractor Rule Comment Period Closes April 28
The DOL's Notice of Proposed Rulemaking — published February 27 to rescind the 2024 rule and restore a modified 2021 framework centered on two core factors (control + opportunity for profit/loss) — closes its 60-day comment period at 11:59 ET April 28. SBA Office of Advocacy estimates the rescission saves small businesses ~$2.31B over 10 years. The 2024 rule technically remains in force for private FLSA litigation, but DOL field staff enforce under the 2008 fact sheet framework.
⚡ Final rule expected later in 2026, typically 30–60 days post-publication. Documentation window is open — but post-Loper Bright, courts aren't bound by DOL's view, so state ABC tests (CA, NJ, IL) still govern.
4
PE Activity
Roofing Contractor Platforms Now Number 56+ — Bolt-On Pace Holds Through April
Per industry tracking cited at IRE, roofing contractor platforms grew from 17 in early 2023 to 56+ by end-2024, with platforms acquiring 134 contractors in 2024 alone. April activity continues the cadence: Confluence Point's EXP platform targeting Northeast/Mid-Atlantic re-roof bolt-ons; Royalty Roofing USA, Ridgeline, Vertex Service Partners (Alpine), and Omnia Exterior Solutions (CCMP) all active. AXIA data shows roofing M&A up 116.7% over six years; Roadmap Advisors confirmed in early April that valuations remain at competitive highs.
⚡ Platform count now larger than the number of MSAs most platforms target. The question is no longer whether to consolidate, but whether your geography is being rolled up around you.
5
Storm Outlook
CSU Issues Below-Average 2026 Atlantic Hurricane Forecast — 13 Named, 6 Hurricanes, 2 Major
Colorado State University's April 9 forecast calls for a slightly below-average 2026 Atlantic season: 13 named storms, 6 hurricanes, 2 major hurricanes, and ACE of 90 (73% of the 1991–2020 average of 123). The team cites a likely ENSO transition to El Niño by peak season, driving elevated wind shear. U.S. major hurricane landfall probability: 32% (vs. 43% long-term avg). Tropical Storm Risk's April 9 forecast is even quieter (12 named, 5 hurricanes, 1 major, ACE 66).
⚡ A quieter season cuts both ways: less storm-driven revenue spike, but more pressure on platforms whose underwriting baked in 2025-style activity. Diligence should normalize the trailing twelve below 2025 ACE, not at it.
Retrospective

Lessons from the Ladder

History this industry learned from — or repeats.

The 2017–2019 Insurance Restoration Roll-Up Aftermath

PE platforms that built their thesis around Hurricane Harvey/Irma/Maria-era insurance restoration revenue ran into the same wall by 2020–2021: when storm-driven volume normalized, the trailing-twelve EBITDA collapsed faster than synergy capture could offset. Several Texas and Florida platforms were quietly recapped or sold below entry multiple.

The platforms that survived had at least 50% replacement-and-reroof revenue independent of storm response — diversification, not weather, was the moat.

🎓 Storm tailwinds compound only if the underlying replacement business compounds independently. Underwrite the calm year, not the active one.

The First Roll-Up Wave: What They Got Wrong (Late 1990s–Early 2000s)

The first institutional wave imposed uniform labor models on hyper-local markets and centralized procurement before systems existed to manage it. They paid premium multiples for revenue they couldn't retain post-transition. Result: value destruction, operator exodus after earnout, and a generation of PE skeptics in the trades.

Platforms that survived share one trait: they bought the operator as much as the business — retaining founders with real equity and building tech infrastructure before they needed it.

🎓 Earnout structures that ignore workforce continuity are the sector's #1 value destroyer.

The Edge
April Tech Spotlight

Things To Watch: Roofr

Each month we spotlight one tool, platform, or technology worth understanding — whether you're an operator, a PE investor evaluating platforms, or both.

If your estimators have been ordering EagleView reports for years, Roofr is the platform increasingly showing up alongside — or instead of — that workflow. Roofr is positioning itself as an end-to-end sales toolbox for roofing contractors: free aerial measurement reports (a direct, freemium challenge to EagleView's paid model), a modern proposal builder, and an integrated CRM that connects to ABC Supply, EagleView, and QuickBooks. The pricing model is the disruption: free tier for measurement and proposals, paid tiers starting at $89–$149/month, against AccuLynx's typical $300–$500+/month and ServiceTitan's $1,225+/month for a 5-crew operation.

What makes Roofr relevant in April 2026 isn't a single launch event but a market-share inflection. Independent industry guides updated in April now consistently rank Roofr in the same conversation as JobNimbus and AccuLynx for residential roofing CRMs, with the highest free-tier adoption rate among the four. For solo operators and shops under $1M, Roofr is replacing a stack that previously required EagleView ($25–$75/report) plus a CRM ($300+/month). For PE platforms, Roofr is a leading indicator of what ServiceTitan paid for Aspire and what Sumeru Equity paid for its $330M majority stake in JobNimbus — software is now cheap enough at the bottom of the market that the moat is integration depth, not access.

The watch-out is the same as every freemium pivot in B2B SaaS: free is a feature acquisition strategy, not a permanent pricing model. Roofs in production at scale eventually need supplier integrations, insurance workflow depth, and call-center dispatch — capabilities where AccuLynx, JobNimbus, and ServiceTitan still lead. The right diligence question on a target running Roofr isn't "how much are they saving on software" but "what's the migration cost when they outgrow it."

What it measures: The variance between estimated job margin (at bid) and actual job margin (at completion), measured in percentage points. A 32% bid that comes in at 28% is a 4-point fade. A 32% bid that comes in at 34% is a 2-point gain. Tracked job-by-job, then rolled up to a trailing-twelve-month portfolio view.

Why PE buyers care: Gain/fade is the cleanest single read on pricing discipline, scope control, and estimating accuracy — three of the hardest things to fix post-close. A platform with consistent fade >3 points is signaling that estimators are bidding on outdated cost data, project managers aren't enforcing scope, or both. Diligence teams now request trailing-twelve gain/fade in Q&A as a standard line item.

April 2026 relevance: April is the first full month carrying the March 23–April 15 Atlas/CertainTeed/TAMKO price increases on installed work. Jobs bid in February at February material pricing are completing in April at April material pricing — the gap shows up directly as fade. Operators who track gain/fade weekly are repricing the next bid; operators who track it quarterly will see Q2 fade balloon in the May close. With 56+ platforms competing on bolt-ons and a quieter storm forecast removing the easy-comp tailwind, fade is now the line item that decides whether your trailing twelve clears LOI.

Watch-out: Gain/fade in isolation can be gamed by padding bids — an estimator who bids at 40% to "guarantee" a 32% finish is delivering false discipline. Pair gain/fade with bid-to-close rate and average ticket: if fade is improving but bid-to-close is collapsing, the estimating team is leaving revenue on the table to protect the metric.

The Square Foot Bottom Line — April 2026

April delivered the most consequential 30 days in roofing distribution history. QXO closed Kodiak on April 1 and announced TopBuild on April 19 — $19B+ of capital deployed in three weeks. Brad Jacobs has now spent ~$30B over 11 months building toward a stated $50B revenue target, and BNP Paribas estimates roughly $6B remains in dry powder. The roofing distribution layer is no longer fragmenting; it is bifurcating between QXO, the Home Depot/SRS combination, and a long tail of regional independents and cooperatives (NEMEON, IDAC) defending against scale.

Inside the contractor market, the March price increases from Atlas, CertainTeed, and TAMKO are now fully landed on April material deliveries. Open bids submitted at February pricing are being installed at April material costs — the gap is showing up directly as fade on every active job. Meanwhile, the DOL's independent contractor rule comment period closes April 28, and the CSU April 9 forecast calls for a below-average 2026 hurricane season (ACE 90 vs. long-term 123). For PE platforms that underwrote 2025-active-season EBITDA, the diligence math is moving against them in real time.

The operators entering Q2 in the strongest position are those who spent April doing three things: tracking gain/fade weekly to isolate where the materials gap is hitting, getting independent contractor documentation in order before the April 28 deadline, and stress-testing platform-level EBITDA against a normalized (not 2025-elevated) storm year. Everyone else is reacting. Know your gain/fade before a buyer's diligence team does — and know what it looks like at 73% of average ACE.

Riddle Answer

Answer: Cap. In roofing, the parapet cap (or coping cap) is the protective metal flashing that sits at the top of a flat-roof parapet wall — compress its design too aggressively and water finds its way in at the joints. In private equity, the cap rate is the income-to-value ratio governing every CRE-adjacent deal — compress it too aggressively in a hot market and the IRR thesis leaks out the back end. Same word. Different elevations — and somehow, the same consequences when you push the geometry past what the structure can hold.
